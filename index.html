How it works

Two identical copies of the same loop play simultaneously. Tape A runs at normal speed. Tape B runs slightly faster — the percentage set by the drift slider. As they play, the offset between them slowly accumulates. When the offset reaches one full loop length, the tapes are back in unison and the cycle begins again.

Use any of the three sources: the built-in default loop (a short rhythmic pattern designed to make phasing effects immediately audible), an uploaded audio file in any browser-supported format, or a microphone recording up to 6 seconds. Spoken syllables, short musical phrases, and rhythmic material all work well.

Sync hold

Hold the K key on your keyboard (or press and hold the K button on mobile) to momentarily run both tapes at the same rate. While held, the offset freezes. Release to resume drift from wherever the offset was. This lets you hold any phase relationship you find interesting — a technique from Reich's live performance practice that most digital phase machine implementations omit.

The original phasing effect in Reich's tape pieces was an accident: two tape machines running at slightly different motor speeds. The sync hold inverts that — it introduces control into an otherwise automatic process, making the performer an active participant in where the phase sits rather than a passive observer of drift.

What to listen for

As the loops drift, the auditory system actively constructs patterns that don't exist in either loop alone. At the mid-phase position (offset ≈ 50% of loop length), the material is most interleaved and the emergent patterns are most complex. Many listeners report unexpected melodies, apparent changes in rhythm, and moments where the two loops seem to merge into a single voice. None of these are present in the source material. They emerge from the relationship between the two signals — and from how the brain organises what it hears.

Loop length matters

Shorter loops phase faster and produce more rapid rhythmic changes. Longer loops drift slowly, with transformations taking several minutes to complete. Try the same drift rate with recordings of different lengths to hear how loop duration shapes the experience. A 0.5-second loop at 1% drift completes a full cycle in about 50 seconds. A 4-second loop at the same drift rate takes about 6.5 minutes.
